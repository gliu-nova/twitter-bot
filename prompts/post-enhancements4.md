Very good progress, the templates are now close to production quality. Make some more minor enhancements and improvements.

Do not redesign the posting format or significantly change the existing structure. Instead, improve the editorial quality of every generated post.

Adopt the mindset of an experienced financial editor reviewing each post before publication.

For every generated post, optimize for these principles in order of importance:

1. Immediate comprehension.
   A reader scrolling X should understand what happened within 2–3 seconds without already knowing the indicator.

2. Every sentence must add new information.
   Do not repeat the headline in the body. Each line should answer a different question:

* What happened?
* Why did it happen?
* Why does it matter?

3. Prefer natural market language.
   Write like a professional market analyst, not a template engine.
   If two phrasings are equally accurate, choose the one a human would naturally write.

4. Prefer observations over predictions.
   State what the data shows.
   Avoid forecasting price or implying certainty unless directly supported by the data.

5. Explain implications, not definitions.
   Do not teach what funding, basis, or open interest are.
   Instead explain why today's change is interesting.

6. When available, enrich posts with contextual information that helps readers understand the significance of the event. Prefer context over additional commentary. Context may include historical rarity (largest in 90 days, highest this month, new all-time high), relationship to current price action (following today's rally/selloff), or comparisons to recent market conditions. Context should be factual and directly supported by the data. Do not invent explanations or force weak historical comparisons.
   Examples:

* Largest move in 30 days.
* Most negative funding this month.
* Highest open interest on record.

Do not force historical context into every post.

7. Avoid weak or redundant wording.

Examples:

Instead of:
"The quote gap widened materially."

Prefer:
"Coinbase and Kraken moved farther apart."

Instead of:
"Carry has flipped against short positioning."

Prefer:
"Funding has flipped below zero, with shorts now paying longs."

Instead of:
"Positioning is becoming heavier."

Prefer:
"Traders are adding leverage."
or
"Open positions continue to build."

8. Make implications informative but appropriately cautious.

Good:

* More leverage is entering the market.
* Long positioning has been reduced.
* Futures demand strengthened relative to spot.
* Price differences between exchanges are widening.

Avoid:

* Price is likely to...
* This confirms...
* Bulls are in control.
* Bears are in control.
* A squeeze is inevitable.

9. Vary sentence structure naturally.

Do not make every post follow identical rhythm.

Sometimes begin with rarity.

Sometimes begin with the observation.

Sometimes combine observation and implication.

The account should feel like it has a consistent voice rather than identical templates.

10. Final editorial checklist.

Before accepting any generated post, verify:

* Would an experienced trader naturally write this?
* Does every sentence provide new information?
* Is the implication supported by the data?
* Is there unnecessary repetition?
* Can the wording be simpler without losing precision?
* Does this teach the reader something useful?
